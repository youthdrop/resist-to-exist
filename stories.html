When Standing Up Becomes “Resisting”

Published by Resist to Exist Campaign

When standing up for yourself becomes a criminal act labeled as "resisting," we have all lost something fundamental. When law enforcement selectively enforces laws against certain communities, it sends a message that rights and protections are not applied equally.

A free society depends on the ability of people to question authority, assert their rights, and hold public officials accountable. Yet when standing up for yourself is redefined as "resisting," something dangerous happens. The line between public safety and the suppression of individual freedom begins to disappear. Across many communities, particularly communities that have historically experienced over-policing, people have learned that exercising their rights can sometimes be interpreted as criminal behavior. Asking why you are being stopped, documenting a police encounter, questioning an officer's actions, refusing consent to a search, or simply failing to comply quickly enough with conflicting commands can result in allegations of resisting or obstructing an officer. In these moments, what should be protected constitutional conduct is transformed into grounds for arrest. The problem becomes even more troubling when enforcement is selective. Laws that are rarely applied in some neighborhoods are aggressively enforced in others. Behaviors that are overlooked when committed by one group become criminalized when committed by another. The result is a two-tiered system of justice where the practical meaning of freedom depends on who you are, where you live, and how law enforcement perceives you. Selective enforcement does not only harm those who are arrested. It harms entire communities. It teaches people that speaking up may carry consequences. It discourages witnesses from reporting misconduct. It undermines trust in public institutions. It creates a culture where fear replaces participation and silence replaces accountability. The issue is larger than any single arrest, any one officer, or any particular community. When laws can be used to punish people for asserting their rights, every person's liberty is placed at risk. Rights only exist when they can be exercised without fear of retaliation. If standing up for yourself can be labeled as resistance, then the protections guaranteed to all of us become conditional rather than universal. This is why accountability matters. Communities deserve transparency, fair treatment, and equal application of the law. Public trust cannot be built through selective enforcement or maintained through fear. It can only be built when constitutional rights are respected and when the people entrusted with enforcing the law are held accountable to the same principles of justice that they are sworn to protect. When standing up becomes resisting, we all lose. We lose confidence in our institutions, we lose faith in equal justice, and we lose a piece of the freedom that belongs to every person. The fight for accountability is not simply about protecting one community—it is about protecting the rights and liberties of everyone.

Organizer Story

Muslah Abdul-Hafeez

Published by Resist to Exist Campaign

After decades of experiencing and witnessing police abuse, Muslah Abdul-Hafeez began advocating for community members who were subjected to police brutality and misconduct.

Officers would often attend community events, such as "Coffee with a Cop," where they interacted with residents in a professional and empathetic manner. However, many of these same officers were later accused of engaging in racial profiling, harassment, and the use of excessive force against community members.

As a result of Muslah's advocacy and the work of his police accountability unit—which trained more than 20 community members to lawfully monitor police activity in the field—he became the target of retaliation by the gang unit and was charged with five counts of Penal Code section 148.

Muslah exercised his right to a jury trial, where officers testified against him. After hearing all of the evidence, the jury acquitted Muslah of all charges.

Despite his acquittal, the retaliation has continued. Muslah alleges that law enforcement has now sought to suppress his First Amendment rights by pursuing a restraining order that would limit his ability to engage in police accountability work, monitor law enforcement activities, and speak publicly about what he believes are ongoing harms caused by police misconduct.

The Cost of Fighting a Charge That Should Not Exist

Published by Resist to Exist Campaign

**The Cost of a Single Stop: Alvina and Kwamane's Story** In March 2025, Alvina and Kwamane were stopped by the police once again. It was not their first encounter with law enforcement. In fact, it was their tenth traffic stop in just six months. According to the couple, each stop was based on a minor pretext used to justify pulling them over. This stop would prove to be the most damaging. After being pulled over, Alvina and Kwamane were searched and arrested. Their vehicle was impounded, leaving them stranded and separated from their transportation. When they attempted to locate their vehicle, they were told by the San Diego Police Department that they did not have possession of the car. It was only after using an AirTag tracking device that they were able to locate it at an impound lot. Recovering their vehicle cost nearly $500 in towing and storage fees. The consequences did not end there. Both Alvina and Kwamane were forced to miss work while dealing with the arrest, vehicle recovery, and court appearances. They had to pay for childcare while attending hearings and trying to navigate the legal system. By the time the ordeal was over, the financial impact on their family had reached an estimated $1,500. Ultimately, the City Attorney declined to file charges. No conviction. No guilty plea. No accountability for the disruption caused to their lives. Yet the costs continued to mount. Beyond the financial burden was the emotional toll. The couple described feeling humiliated, anxious, and traumatized by the experience. Being handcuffed, transported from their community, processed through the criminal justice system, and forced to defend themselves against charges that were ultimately rejected left lasting emotional scars. The public also paid a price. Police officers spent time conducting the stop, searching the vehicle, making arrests, writing reports, transporting the couple, processing them through booking procedures, and appearing in court. Conservatively estimated, these activities cost taxpayers approximately $3,000 in officer time, overtime, administrative costs, transportation, and booking expenses. Taken together, one stop resulted in an estimated $4,500 in direct costs borne by both the family and taxpayers—without a single charge being filed. This story is not simply about dollars and cents. It is about the human cost of unnecessary enforcement. It is about parents losing wages, families struggling to recover impound fees, children experiencing disruption, and community members carrying the trauma of being treated as suspects rather than neighbors. When a stop ends with no charges filed, the question remains: Who pays for the damage that has already been done? Alvina and Kwamane did. Their children did. And so did the taxpayers of San Diego.
